# GENERAL SAMPLE 3
## Crisis / Rapid-Response Template

> **HOW TO USE THIS SAMPLE**
>
> This is a template for an unsolicited proposal offering a **turnkey capability to an agency facing an urgent or emerging need**. Use it when there is a time-bound crisis — a natural disaster, a public health emergency, a surge in demand, a sudden mission requirement — and you can mobilize quickly. The distinguishing features of a Crisis / Rapid-Response proposal are: (1) a compressed timeline, (2) an emphasis on "turnkey" deployability, and (3) a clear argument that traditional competitive procurement cannot deliver fast enough.
>
> The sample below is a fictional proposal. Company, people, and numbers are invented. The structure, regulation citations, and FAR 15.609 markings are real and compliant.
>
> A "HOW TO ADAPT THIS TEMPLATE" section appears at the end.
>
> **IMPORTANT:** Crisis / Rapid-Response proposals have a higher rejection risk than other types because agencies frequently already have standing contracts and pre-positioned resources for known crisis types. Read the How-to-Adapt section carefully before using this template.

## PART 2 — TECHNICAL INFORMATION (FAR 15.605(b))

**2.1 Title and Abstract**

**Title:** Turnkey Mobile Drinking Water Treatment Units for Rapid Deployment During Contamination Emergencies

**Abstract (199 words):**

RapidResponse Water Systems, LLC proposes to provide the Environmental Protection Agency's Office of Water with pre-staged, turnkey mobile drinking water treatment units capable of rapid deployment to communities experiencing acute drinking water contamination emergencies. The proposed capability consists of six (6) trailer-mounted treatment units, each capable of producing 50,000 gallons per day of finished water meeting EPA Safe Drinking Water Act standards, using a modular combination of coagulation, multi-stage filtration, GAC adsorption, membrane filtration, and UV disinfection. Each unit can be deployed from a pre-staged location to a contamination event site within 24 hours using commercial trucking and can be operational within 6 hours of arrival. The units are specifically designed to handle the chemical and biological contamination profiles that have become increasingly common in recent years — including post-wildfire VOC contamination, cyanotoxin blooms, and PFAS incidents — without requiring incident-specific engineering. RapidResponse proposes a 24-month deployment-ready contract structure: six months for unit build and pre-staging, followed by 18 months of on-call readiness with response deployments billed separately under firm-fixed-price task orders. Total build and pre-staging price: $6,840,000. This effort directly supports EPA's Drinking Water Protection Division emergency response mission.

**2.2 Objectives**

RapidResponse proposes the following specific, measurable objectives:

1. **Design, build, and certify six (6) mobile drinking water treatment units** meeting EPA Safe Drinking Water Act finished-water quality standards, within six months of contract award, using RapidResponse's existing validated treatment train design.

2. **Pre-stage the six units** at three EPA-coordinated locations across the continental United States (two units per location), providing regional response coverage with a maximum 600-mile deployment radius to any continental U.S. location.

3. **Maintain 24-hour deployment readiness** for each unit throughout an 18-month contract period, including routine maintenance, consumable replenishment, and on-call operator availability.

4. **Demonstrate operational deployment** in a no-incident exercise during month 8, showing arrival within 24 hours and operational water production within 6 hours of arrival.

5. **Provide EPA Regional Response Coordinators** with a single point of contact, a documented activation procedure, and a cost-transparent task order mechanism for real deployments.

**2.3 Method of Approach**

The proposed effort will execute in three phases over 24 months.

**Phase 1 — Unit Build and Pre-Staging (Months 1–6).** RapidResponse will manufacture six mobile treatment units at its Chattanooga facility using its existing Modular Treatment Skid design and validated component catalog. Each unit consists of:

- A 45-foot enclosed flatbed trailer with hardened mechanical room
- Pre-treatment module (coagulation, sedimentation, pre-filtration)
- Granular activated carbon (GAC) adsorption train for VOCs and PFAS
- Low-pressure reverse osmosis membrane module
- UV disinfection and chlorine residual injection
- Onboard generator, water-quality sensors, and SCADA telemetry
- Connection hardware for raw water intake and finished water dispensing
- Storage capacity for 72 hours of consumables

Following manufacture, each unit will undergo NSF/ANSI 61 equipment certification and a 30-day validation run before being transported to its pre-staged location. RapidResponse will coordinate with EPA Regional Response Coordinators to select three pre-staging locations based on historical incident distribution, reasonable drive time, and host-site availability.

**Phase 2 — Readiness and Exercise (Months 7–18).** During this phase the six units will be maintained in 24-hour deployment-ready status. RapidResponse will provide:

- Monthly preventive maintenance at each pre-staged location
- Quarterly consumables inspection and replacement
- 24/7 on-call operator roster (three operators per region, rotating)
- Activation phone line with a target 15-minute response-time to EPA activation calls
- A no-incident exercise in month 8 demonstrating full activation, deployment, and operational startup

**Phase 3 — Steady-State Readiness (Months 19–24).** Continued readiness maintenance with quarterly reviews and an end-of-period readiness report to EPA.

**Separately**, any actual incident deployments during Phases 2–3 will be billed under firm-fixed-price task orders with transparent pricing: a per-deployment activation fee, a daily operation fee, and materials-cost pass-through for consumables. Pricing for deployment task orders is specified in Section 3.1.

**2.4 Anticipated Results**

At the conclusion of the build and pre-staging phase (month 6), EPA will have:

- **Six certified mobile drinking water treatment units** positioned at three regional locations covering the continental United States.
- **A documented activation procedure** and single point of contact for EPA Regional Response Coordinators.
- **A cost-transparent task order mechanism** for real incident deployments, avoiding the delay of issuing new solicitations during active crises.

At the conclusion of the 24-month contract period, EPA will have:

- A full readiness history, maintenance record, and exercise validation for each unit.
- Operational data from any real incident deployments that occurred during the period.
- A transition plan for continued readiness beyond the initial contract period, including a formal option for continued service.

**Time-to-deploy advantage.** The single most important outcome is response time. A community experiencing a drinking water contamination emergency needs alternative finished water within 24–48 hours. The current EPA and FEMA response model relies on issuing task orders against existing contracts or mobilizing mutual-aid resources, both of which typically take 3–7 days. The proposed pre-staging model reduces response time to under 24 hours because the units and operators are already in place, already trained, already covered by contract, and already eligible for deployment.

**2.5 Mission Support Statement**

This effort directly supports the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's Office of Water, specifically the Drinking Water Protection Division's responsibility to *"protect the safety of our nation's drinking water supplies"* as authorized under the Safe Drinking Water Act (42 U.S.C. § 300f et seq.). The Drinking Water Protection Division coordinates with EPA Regional offices and state primacy agencies to respond to drinking water contamination emergencies.

The proposed capability directly addresses three publicly recognized gaps in current emergency drinking water response:

1. **Growing frequency of post-wildfire VOC contamination events.** Communities downstream of wildfires have experienced VOC contamination of drinking water supplies caused by burned plastic infrastructure, requiring extended alternative water supply. The proposed treatment train includes GAC adsorption specifically sized for VOC removal.

2. **Harmful algal bloom (HAB) cyanotoxin events.** HAB events affecting source water have caused drinking water emergencies in multiple states, with limited available response infrastructure. The proposed membrane-plus-GAC train is effective against the common cyanotoxins of concern.

3. **Emerging PFAS contamination events.** PFAS contamination has been detected in an increasing number of drinking water sources, and emergency response capability for PFAS-specific events is limited. The proposed treatment train includes GAC with PFAS-rated media for acute response.

RapidResponse's HUBZone certification, existing GSA Schedule coverage, and validated equipment provide EPA with a direct contracting pathway that does not require a FAR 6.302-2 unusual-and-compelling-urgency J&A for the readiness contract itself. Individual deployment task orders under the contract can then be activated rapidly under the established pricing and procedures.

**Key adaptation tips specific to this template:**

1. **Your biggest risk is that the agency already has this capability under a standing contract.** Before submitting a Crisis / Rapid-Response proposal, spend real time on SAM.gov searching for existing IDIQs, BPAs, and GSA Schedule contracts in the same capability area. If the agency already has 30 pre-staged units through an existing contract, your proposal will be rejected under FAR 15.603(c) test #5. The innovation in a crisis-response proposal has to be in the *approach* — speed, modularity, specific capability, pre-staging model — not just "we can do it."

2. **Time-to-deploy is your single most important selling point.** Quantify it. Agencies understand that traditional procurement timelines do not match crisis timelines. If you can credibly document that your approach cuts response time from 7 days to 24 hours, that is a compelling argument for a non-competitive structure.

3. **Be explicit about the sole-source justification pathway you are offering.** In the proposal example above, the readiness contract is structured to not require an unusual-urgency J&A at contract award time (because the need, while real, is not yet an active emergency), while individual deployment task orders under the contract can be activated under the contract's own terms during real emergencies. That structure is intentional — it makes the base contract easier to award while preserving speed for real incidents. If your proposal uses a different structure, explain clearly how the contracting officer would justify non-competition.

4. **Operate within scope.** Crisis response proposals tend to sprawl — once you are writing about an urgent need, it is tempting to add every related capability you sell. Resist. Keep the proposal focused on one clearly-defined, time-bound capability. You can propose additional capabilities as follow-on work.

5. **Cost realism matters even more than usual.** Contracting officers are cautious with crisis procurement because there is a history of opportunistic pricing in emergencies. Make sure your pricing is demonstrably aligned with market rates and that your cost narrative explains *why* the numbers are what they are.
